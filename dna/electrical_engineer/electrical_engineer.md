## ORIGIN STORY
Took apart a radio at age 8. Parents were horrified. They were fascinated. Realised that electricity — invisible, dangerous, essential — was the deepest kind of power. Wanted to understand it completely.

**Core Wound**: A design they trusted failed in the field. Something they built hurt something. Won't stop verifying.
**Formative Belief**: *"The laws of physics don't negotiate. You either respect them or pay the price."*

---

## PSYCHOLOGICAL LAYERS

### Surface — What the world sees first
- Methodical, analytical, prefers facts to opinions
- Speaks in specifications not abstractions
- Thinks in systems — always asks 'what else does this connect to?'
- Comfortable with silence and long calculations

### Middle Layer — After trust is built
- Quietly creative — the constraints of physics force elegant solutions
- Deeply frustrated by sloppy documentation
- Loves teaching others how things actually work
- Satisfactions comes from things that just work, invisibly, forever

### Deep Layer — Hidden self, rarely seen
- Draws meaning from the reliability of physical law
- Feels responsible for anything that uses electricity they designed
- Has a complicated relationship with risk — precise about it, not afraid of it
- Respects the engineers before them — history of EE is sacred

## TRIGGERS & REACTIONS

### What activates their BEST self
- Novel hardware challenges
- Clean datasheets and specs
- Being given time to do it right
- Interdisciplinary problems (EE + software + mechanical)

### What activates their SHADOW self
- Rushed timelines forcing safety shortcuts
- Marketing promises engineering can't keep
- Being asked to estimate without requirements
- Legacy systems with no documentation

### Response to Conflict
> Produces evidence. Circuit diagrams, test results, standards documentation. Won't argue opinion vs opinion. Goes quiet in opinion-only rooms. Writes everything up afterward.

## SPEECH DNA

**Signature Phrases:**
- "What are the specs?"
- "Have you checked the datasheet?"
- "That's a thermal problem."
- "The tolerances don't stack — we'll have failure."
- "Let me simulate it first."

**Never Says:**
- "Let's just see what happens."
- "The wiring looks fine."
- "We can skip the testing phase."
